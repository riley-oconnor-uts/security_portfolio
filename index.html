Reflection

Reflective Statement

1. Engage with stakeholders to identify a problem

There were a good handful of stakeholders I involved myself with in the course of the studio. Mostly these were guests of the facilitating staff and industry professionals, who introduced us to concepts, tools, and methods that we used in solving a problem, whether given by them or self-defined.

Any of the guests could be mentioned, Ruben gave us problems such as how to find something within a program when the element itself is never explicitly stated, this was solved through the process of program reverse engineering and, while I don’t consider myself skilled at it, I was able to solve this issue with a few of the challenges he provided.


Things like the presentations on web vulnerabilities and tools used in penetration testing served to engage us with stakeholders with a given problem and associate our topic with the business aspects, meaning we had to evaluate what the needs of the stakeholder were and what specifically they desired from the presentation and the details they requested.

Practical experience was valued above all else within this studio, so any involvement with stakeholders was generally involved a certain level of technical understanding to grasp how different elements could be used to leverage a system, demoing of exploits and explaining vulnerabilities was not only done by students, but by staff and guests as well, properly understanding what was being discussed, taking notes and replicating the findings were important in solving the issuing and furthering progress.

2. Apply design thinking or systems thinking to respond to a defined or newly identified problem

Research and understanding up a large portion of the time in offensive security, creativity, and proper research is needed to find a suitable method of penetrating a system. No implementation is the same and as such, it should be expected that each system has different methods of or mitigation towards intrusion.

Design thinking is one way of approaching the problem at hand and can be generally applied to the process of solving a given problem in a system. Using my work on Kioptrix3 I hope to show how I applied a Design Thinking style thought process to solving the issue of bypassing the login system on a system where I had no hints to produce the username/password.


As I had a defined problem I had not figure out how to solve it, the most obvious solution was to find the login, doing so would allow me to log in and further penetrate the system, however, there were no files or scripts running that stored the user’s login information within and therefore that solution was not possible. The next idea was to try some form of SQL injection, meaning I could skip finding the username/password and bypass the login by manipulating how the system handled database queries, there are a number of ways this can be done in a login prompt so I used some of the simple injection methods to get through, on testing these did not work so I had to try with another method. The method that worked was found through using provided resources to gain connection directly to the server’s web shell, allowing me to navigate the system freely without logging in, it was not the simplest solution and took me a few tests to get it working, but I successfully solved that problem within the system.



In the second week I developed a problem statement to partially define the work I would be doing over the period of the studio, it went as such:
With the constant discovery of new vulnerabilities older systems are increasingly more likely to be susceptible to potentially compromising attacks
In working through multiple boxes I continued to find this claim to be true, obviously, this is because it is a very realistic and general statement, as a problem statement for the work I have completed it is suitable and I did not see the need to change it.

I don’t believe design thinking or systems thinking were big influencers on my work, but their process was natural to the solving of problems within any system, being supplementary to the overall goal of any vulnerable machine.

Just to mention, but of course design thinking was used in developing this website and solving the issues with setting it up and migrating it between static and generated static platforms, but that is external to the overall goal of the studio and is detailed in the week 1 and week 2 blog posts so I only see worth in mentioning it rather than detailing it explicitly.

3. Apply technical skills to develop, model and/or evaluate designs

Starting with no prior experience, in the course of four weeks I built a foundation of offensive security skills and was able to obtain root access on six vulnerable machines:

  • Web for Pentester
  • Deloittes Piper
  • Kioptrix1
  • Kioptrix3
  • PwnLab:init
  • Curling
    Using my understanding of computer systems, vulnerabilities, research materials and provided tools I was able to evaluate systems for their flaws and solve the challenge set for each system. I used a standard process for the infiltration of each system, starting from analysing and enumerating the system for its points of access, followed by researching the potential ways I could take advantage of an entry point to escalate myself within the system followed by executing the attacks I thought might work and repeating the process. While this is a simplification of the process, it roughly describes how I, or any attacker, should escalate within a system.





I used a good variety of tools, with each serving a particular purpose in executing my attack, whether it be nmap or dirbuster for the sake of enumerating a system, or Metasploit and ExploitDB to find exploits, I needed to learn and use a handful of programs, resources and packages to automate and assist me in gaining access to vulnerable machines.

Is my knowledge extensive, of course not, but I have built the fundamentals for working professionally in similar environments. Like anything, achieving good technical skills in security requires a solid understanding and a particular way of thinking, I’ve fostered this way of thinking about problems and experienced a good variety of situations to best characterise the types of vulnerabilities a system might have.

4. Demonstrate effective collaboration and communication skills

A consistent deliverable in all of the weeks was some level of presentation, meaning for students to present some form of findings in an understandable sense to students and staff of varying skill levels, I presented on a couple of topics, the BEEF tool, Flash vulnerabilities, and the open-day presentations. I would say I presented anywhere between well and adequately in my presentations, suffering slightly from timing restraints and not explaining in a business friendly manner. These tasks were mostly collaborative, working in groups of four to produce and present the work, I worked well with my group as we were familiar with each other, so I had no issues in that regard.

Sprint reflections within the student group and free-for-alls were an important part in gauging participation and progress among the student group, not really for the staff, but for the students. The reflections were slow due to most not holding to the timing requirements, but allowed everyone to understand each other’s progress together, by comparison, the free-for-alls were much better for in-depth discussion but did not provide the class-wide understanding.


I worked considerably with other students to help solve problems or roadblocks found when completing vulnerable machines, more experienced students were a great help in showing new resources, some were tools, like CyberChef, while others were commands and options which I used a lot in enumerating systems, such as sudo -l.

I spoke a lot with Larry and Darsh to understand the requirements of each week, asking how things should be formatted, what resources are suitable and voicing my progress on tasks or opinion on topics. I did this to ensure I was staying on track and completing the tasks in an agreeable manner, the two gave extremely positive feedback and I took it to heart, modifying what I was doing in the case of the website, or utilising new things they had suggested in the case of week three’s vulnerable machines.

5. Conduct critical self, peer and group review and performance evaluation

I feel pretty good about everything at the end of the studio, I’ve mentioned this before, but I feel my goal was achieved early into the studio and my pace was incredibly fast, but I slowed down a lot after that as I tried more challenging things to further my learning. Also mentioned, but I felt very nervous starting the studio and was extremely pressured for at least the first two weeks, looking back at that I am now more comfortable with my work and rather proud of what I achieved.

I haven’t the slightest reasonable complaint about anyone from the studio, staff, students or guests, they were all extremely accommodating and helpful. Some of the people who started at similar levels to me showed much more competence than I have, maybe because they focused on different aspects or challenges than I did. There are plenty of students in the studio there were a few students I did not speak with, this is my fault and the only times I could see their progress was during the sprint reflections at the end of the week, of those I did speak with, everyone seemed to have made a lot of progress in the studio.

I have specific reflections on the individual sprints and tasks in each weekly post, these capture my opinions at the time well, I’ve had positive and negative thoughts on how I or my group performed in any given task, but I would claim we completed any required task to a good depth and showed a lot of positive growth between the first and final week.

The course itself was interesting and fun, it didn’t suit the design of the summer studio fully and suffered only the slightest from not properly explained deliverables, but these were rectified through discussion and evaluation of the contents over the course of each sprint, so that was normal and to be expected.

Conclusion

What have I learned? Coming into this course I was rushed, unprepared and nervous beyond belief, I was trying to keep myself above the abundant amounts of content provided and fulfill the high standard I had set for myself. Did I achieve this? Undoubtedly, my goal at the beginning was not complex, I just wanted to try and get all the way into a system, I found within the first two weeks that doing so wasn’t nearly as difficult as I had envisioned it to be and quickly achieved my goal. I learned a lot, and after completing my goal I continued to learn, in less of a frenzy than before, I picked up a few new skills during this time and tried a few things that might have been a touch too difficult for me, but regardless I think I learned from them.

I don’t think I pushed myself nearly as hard as I could have, but I set a realistic goal and worked hard to get it done, I plan to work further on this in the long term and the push to learn this much as quick as I can has given me more momentum than I could have had any other way. What was most important to this was probably the large number of people who wholeheartedly wanted to help and shared their insights, methods, and knowledge with me, its been great.

Week 4 Reflection

As the fourth week of the summer studio (25/2/19 - 3/3/19) this post and its accompanying post cover the work of the final deliverables, the contents of this post will likely be much shorter than the previous three posts on account of the contents being split between this post, a final reflection post on the studio and a formal report document (which will be detailed further).

Because it was the final week, the work we delivered should reflect the work we have put in over the course of the month and present a final product as tangible evidence of our efforts. To give a proof that was consistent between all of the students it was requested that we each produce a report on the breaking into a standard machine, starting from boot, with the ultimate goal being to obtain root access on the machine. The machines used for this were suggested to be from the active Hack the Box resources to remove the ability to use guides and as many hints as possible, this replicated, mostly, the experience of breaking into a real-world machine.

Further, an open-day was held on Friday of the fourth week (1/3/19) to showcase the work studio members produced during the month to interested parties, professionals, staff, and other students. This served as a wrap-up celebration for the studio and to promote the work that was put in. Students from all of the different studio topics hosted presentations and displays of their work in the designated rooms for their topic, allowing for anyone to roam between the different areas.

Reverse Engineering Exercises

A series of reverse engineering challenges were provided to us on Monday by Ruben, who presented on the topic that day. Of all the contents over the past four weeks, I would claim that this one had the highest learning curve and was the most difficult to understand. There were four challenges provided of increasing difficulty, I completed three of the challenges, but will only be detailing two of them to completion because I forgot to take notes on how to complete the third and could not replicate my steps. The resource hosting the challenges is at http://uts-cs.securitygrounds.org/reverse-engineering/0x00/index.html.

0x01

This was the first reverse engineering challenge I ever attempted, it was easy to understand at a basic level. When the script provided was run it would output the following:


This wasn’t all too helpful in finding the flag that existed somewhere in the program but using Binary Ninja you could see the space in the memory this ASCII image was stored using Binary Ninja.


Binary Ninja allows for the traversal of program memory, looking through the list of functions I found the main function, which calls function1. In function1 it can be seen that there is an iterating loop that ends at the value 0x4, being iterated 0x1 every loop, and then calls function2.


Function2 is the final function and stores a string value, identifiable by the 0x0 byte at the end, once the hex is converted to character constant the flag string is revealed.


This challenge was simple and served to familiarise me with the layout and navigation of functions and memory.

0x02

This challenge was a step up from the previous and required some trial and error to solve. There was no hint in the output of the file, but the title of the file hinted at the use of some variety of crypto technique in the process.

Similarly to the previous challenge, I went straight to the main function, there was a string value, however, its output was not in ASCII, instead storing hex values and therefore not providing the proper flag yet.

Further into the function, it can be seen that each character in the string value is XORed with the value “45” to produce the output since XOR is reversible inputting the same value a second time would produce the proper flag.

Some confusion came when attempting to XOR the string, using CyberChef I converted the string from hex and XORed using the key 0x45, however, the output did not come out in a human-readable way. After some testing, I figured the output was reversed in some way. If the input was entered per-line of the string value in reverse the output resulted in the flag.

0x03

By far the most challenging of the programs I accessed. When run the program would output an application menu asking for an option, regardless of the option entered it would return a string stating a Meterpreter session had been opened.


Looking at the program in Binary Ninja it could be seen that the application menu was taking and processing an input, the function that caught my eye was the decrypt_flag function, which was storing and moving a secret number value. Using gdb you could manipulate the function to produce the secret number value, which, once entered into the program, would spit out an encrypted version of the flag. I was not able to reproduce this.


I did the third challenge with David, he was much better at understanding what was going on in the programs and his knowledge of C was the main point in obtaining the flag in the third challenge, I mostly offered cursory things, offering ideas and pointing out locations of different things.

Learning


Reverse engineering is really hard and requires a lot of knowledge of the fundamentals of any programming language and assembly, even though these were basic challenges they were very difficult for me to complete, even with the help of people who knew the C language. Even if this isn’t something I see myself doing I found the knowledge regarding assembly to be not only interesting but also useful in the understanding of a program. I don’t see myself doing something like this often, but it is worthwhile to have the experience and keep it at the back of my mind.

Vulnerable Machine

I’ll just note some of my experiences with doing the vulnerable machine without noting any of the specifics as to how I obtained the flags or the commands I used. I obtained the user and root flags for the Hack the Box machine titled Curling, finding the solution took a fair while and, as with many things, seemed easy in retrospect despite the large amount of difficulty I had. I learned a few new things doing this machine, which should be noted in my report.

I chose to do Curling based on its difficulty score (easy) and the fact not many other people seemed to be doing it, I want to do a more challenging machine, but I am leaving that for my own spare time, without any constraints.

Open-day

Friday (1/3/19) was the final day of the summer studio classes and served as a display of all the effort students had put in since the beginning of the month, there were a wide variety of subjects being presented on across different rooms in Building 11 of UTS starting at 1pm. I arrived at 10am to organise with my group and hang around to see what everyone had planned for the presentation time.

My group planned to present using a demo of the Deloittes box and show the functionality of BEEF, basically show some of the simpler things we had occupied ourselves with over the past four weeks. The range of topics we could show was rather limited because of the disclosure agreements that we had to abide by, limiting us from talking about Hack the Box boxes or the real-world pen-test. I had wanted to have slides, but doing so would mean we couldn’t show the demo without taking up too much time.

We set up in a corner of the room early in the day and had a bit of difficulty setting up the BEEF demo because Nick arrived barely before 1pm, causing the BEEF demo to be set up soon after 1:30pm. Everyone, from every group, was extremely energetic setting up and presenting, the mood was kept high, there was a mix between business style presentations and demos of boxes or tools. It was strange setting up as, though it felt very busy it was at the same time relaxed in some way.

I really enjoyed the day, just as an opportunity to just relax and keep a good mood, but we didn’t stay very long and only presented a couple of times on account of some group members wanting to leave well in advance.

Final Thoughts

It really has been a great experience to try all of these new things that I have never known to try, UTS just doesn’t offer subjects on the offensive security type subjects to the kind of practical level that the summer studio has. I know Darsh, Jai, Larry and Luke all put a lot of work into making this subject work and develop it and the experience has provided me an array of new skills and knowledge that I hope to develop some time in the future.

As for where to go from here, I plan to learn more in the future as a hobby endeavour starting with completing more active boxes, I don’t intend to go to B-Sides this year, but some time in the future I would like to indulge and participate in similar events for the sake of my own curiousity.

Basically, its been a whole lot of fun, I have learned far more than I had expected, achieved more than I had thought I would and want to keep trying.

Thanks.

Week 3 Reflection

This post covers the contents of what I learned and did in the third week of the security studio between Monday (18/2/19) and Sunday (24/2/19). In building on the previous week, which focused on breaking into a web app. The third week iterates on this by taking the skills refined in web penetration to obtain root access on a system, allowing for full privileged access to the system.

The deliverables for the week were twofold, first, to present on a tool for breaking into a system in groups, and secondly, to either pen-test a real-world case or obtain boot-to-root access on vulnerable machines. I chose the latter in this case due to comfort and obtained root access on Kioptrix 1, Kioptrix 1.2 and Piper with attempts made to obtain root on FristiLeaks 1.3 and PwnLab: init.

Ultimately I didn’t complete as many vulnerable machines as I had wanted and would like to have done more, but I could not due to time constraints, for the sake of documentation I will record what I did on each machine (except Piper) to illustrate my process and the issues I faced or what stopped me from progressing. Most of the content of the page will be regarding my experience with vulnerable machines as I believe it to be the most important and worthwhile content to document.

Guest Speakers

On Wednesday (20/2/19) three industry professionals from Deloittes presented on the topic of practical red-teaming. My main take-away from their presentation was the practical thinking in regards to attempting system penetration and the tools they use to break physical security. Things like rubber duckies and RFID duplication (or things of the like) were tools I had not heard of before, seeing their designs and purposes was interesting from a hardware standpoint.

I think the occupation of red-teaming or similar jobs seem very difficult and stressful due to the skills required and tight time limits, but it is interesting nonetheless to hear stories from industry professionals about their methods and process. I don’t have any intention of making a foray into red-teaming as I don’t want to develop my skills in that direction, but I do enjoy hearing about the process and interesting stories from those that have experience, whether it be from conferences of casual conversation.

Tool Presentation

This presentation consisted of finding, researching and presenting information on a tool used for breaking into machines, the presentation was 6-7 minutes in length with our group consisting of four members, one of whom could not make it for the presentation. The topic we chose was Beef, which is natively installed on Kali Linux, we chose this as our topic because the features provided by Beef seemed fun.


Beef is a tool for executing a number of web attacks through the Beef client, which hosts a hook between a compromised host and the client. The range of attacks that can be executed depends on the host and the local attack database, the attacks range from malicious to mundane and are a lot of fun to play around with, we managed to set up hooks between our sessions and tested some of the features just to play around as well as managed to send phones calls to each other’s phones through Beef. The key requirement to get Beef hooked was to have a user execute a dynamically generated script (generally through some variety of XSS) which would act as the hooking agent for Beef.

Our presentation was split into four sections, how it works, what can be done with it, how it can be prevented and a live demonstration. I wrote the first section and introduced, I like to keep my presentations more slide focused, with the slides providing the main information while I provide more insights or auxiliary information to the topic. I likely spoke for the least time among my group, though that was very much planned as I expected the demonstration to take more time than expected, which it did. I feel I spoke on the topic well, but the core of my information was definitely from the slides rather than from my speaking, in terms of communicating information in a stakeholder friendly manner I was probably a little to technical on some aspects regarding the hooking mechanism.


The most fun from this presentation was actually the research and usage of Beef, we didn’t take it all too seriously and more wanted to just have fun with what felt like a silly tool, that isn’t to say the tool can’t be used maliciously, but it definitely has an aspect of fun to its design and capabilities.

The presentations of other students were useful as I did end up using or at least exploring some of the tools other students mentioned, most notably CyberChef (n1474335 2019) which was presented as a general tool for all kinds of web features. I didn’t go too far into the app, but I did use it for base64 encoding and decoding.

Boot to Root

These are all vulnerable machines from abatchy’s (2017) list of vulnerable virtual machines, I did not attempt all of them, choosing them at my leisure, though I did not want to do too many from the Kioptrix series. I was able to gain root access on three machines, two of which are detailed and attempted a total of five machines.

A lot of the details for how I explored each VM are similar because my process for enumeration and testing is rather simplistic, but I learned a couple of new tools over the process and implemented them as needed, they are detailed in the final section.

Kioptrix1

This was the first VM I tried in the week and it was the easiest to gain root access on. My first step was to use netdiscover to find the IP of the target system and then I used nmap to check the running services and their associated ports. There were quite a number of open ports so I had quite a few avenues of attack to check.


There were no interesting pages to check with dirb, with the noteworthy ones being locked behind 403 errors.


As this was after learning about SSL’s heartbleed exploit I went straight to metasploit and targeted the server to see what would be returned after the execution of heartbleed. This worked and returned an email, but no password, meaning I did not gain any useful information.


When searching the particular version of SSL I found an old exploit called “OpenFuck” (Spabam 2003) that, when the architecture was specified, would use a buffer overflow to grant root access, unfortunately it was out of date, so with a bit more searching I found a guide to update it at https://github.com/heltonWernik/OpenLuck (Helton 2018). Once updated the code would not execute with my desired architecture, the guide said to use 0x6a, but on searching I found there was one for Debian and another for Ubuntu, using 0x6b worked.


The shell this produced had root privilege and I was able to execute commands.

Kioptrix3

The steps to complete Kioptrix3 took a long time to do and was a hard VM to get root access. The ports reported open from nmap were 22 and 80, meaning I could SSH in and that there was a web server. The dirb for this IP returned a PHP admin page and a login page for something called Lotus CMS. In a post on the blog section of the website, there was a username for user loneferret, I noted this down. While there was a comments section I didn’t think an XSS attack would be useful.


Looking at Lotus CMS I noted that it was not an up to date version as newer versions of the CMS used a slightly more modern design, thinking this I searched metasploit and found a command execution script for Lotus CMS. When executed with the URI “/“ this gave me a CLI through meterpreter.



My first thought was to explore the directory, however, meterpreter did not act like a normal CLI, so it took some adjusting. At the time my directory was /home/www/kioptrix3.com. I navigated to data/users/admin.dat which had the contents “|318d8dd409db395f0317efa71b3bad13e1fb9857|administrator|bla@bla.com“, this ended up not being used. Similarly, there was a salt in /data/config that I did not use or explore the uses for.

Under the gallery directory, I found a config file with the details for the SQL database root:fuckeyou. These did not work for Lotus CMS, but did for the PHP admin. This provided an interface to browse the database, which contained two sets of passwords, one for the local users loneferret and dreg, and another for host logins, which I assume were for logging in from particular hosts, such as loopback and root.



Using a tool I had heard of earlier in the week, hashcat, I cracked the hashes and obtained the passwords for the two users.


These logins did not work for Lotus CMS and after a while, I remembered that SSH was open on port 22, which I used to SSH into the loneferret user with loneferret:starwars. Loneferret’s home directory had a shell script and a readme which stated to use sudo ht, when I did this an error was repeated, to fix this I had to issue the command TERM=xterm, which I guess enabled XTERM on the shell.



Since I now had super-user privilege I figured I could edit the sudoers file, which would already contain a configuration for sudo ht. I added the root shell /bin/sh and tried to do the same for the web user, but couldn’t figure a use for it.


Issuing sudo /bin/sh gave me a root shell.

PwnLab

This was by far the most difficult machine I tried, this was due to the number of tools I needed to use that I was not acquainted with to progress through what felt like a very narrow process.

Obviously, the first thing I did was issue netdiscover to determine the target IP and then use nmap to view the open ports and their associated services.


The running services were Apache, RPC, and MySQL, on referencing these with ExploitDB I only found a single Apache exploit, but it did not offer any insight into the system. My next check was to use dirb, which returned directories at /server-status and /images, but I could not access either.


I did not know what to do next as there didn’t seem to be a vulnerability I could use or a webpage I could exploit excepting the fact the site used PHP to parse certain pages. On searching the internet for vulnerability analysis tools I found Nikto, which, when executed returned a page that could be used for storing basic configurations.


Attempting to access the page normally did not work, but one PHP exploit I found called “Local File Include” could be used through PHP to view local files that aren’t displayed client side. While the standard configurations for this did not work I went through a list on HighOn.Coffee (2016) of exploitation methods, with the third one allowing me access to the config file. The method itself involved converting the desired directory into Base64 to bypass the server’s filter and display the results in Base64.


The config page provided the login details and table name for the MySQL database, however, there was no login page on the web server. Attempting to use mysql on Kali showed that there was a package installed for CLI usage of a remote MySQL database, which when logged into provided me the usernames and passwords for three users. The package itself was somewhat confusing and not self-explanatory, taking me a little while to maneuver to the user table.


Once the password for user kent was decoded I logged in and was able to upload a file, the file itself was limited to an image file type and when a file with a false extension was uploaded it would also fail. I could not figure out how to bypass this and stopped attempting to access the system at this point. I would say I made reasonable progress and was not entirely disappointed in not being able to get root access.

Fristi

This VM was somewhat of a pain to complete due to the way the stages of penetration were designed to be somewhat unconventional and confusing, for me at least, I worked on the VM with Oliver. Due to my somehow breaking the VM and any other Fristi copy through some misconfiguration of the networking that I could not rectify I did not get any artefacts for the VM and could only get to uploading a reverse shell before the networking stopped working.

In this particular VM the IP address was already provided, so I went straight to determining the active ports, which returned port 80. On trying to access the index.html page there was nothing interesting to note, so we used Dirb to find any common directories on the site. The result of this was a robots.txt file providing the directory for three hidden directories named /coke, /sisi and /beer.

Through some creative thinking, Oliver figured Fristi was a drink and accessed a directory at /fristi, which lead to a login page. This following step was confusing for us and took a little while to figure out, we were provided a username, a long base64 string and an image encoded in base64. By my thinking, we might have to decode the image to find some hidden string or decode the long string to find the password as text, however, none of these worked.

After some searching, we found a tool to interpret base64 as images, using this we found the password, which was a long string of upper and lower case k’s, when entered into the login prompt with the username we were presented with an upload tool. The tool placed restrictions on file uploads such that only images could be uploaded, in retrospect, the best way to test this would have been to check the source code, however, we just tested different simple extension spoofing methods, with the simplest working. When a file was uploaded with a different extension it would upload the file regardless of the contents, the file I tried to upload was a PHP reverse shell.


Once I set up netcat as a listener I executed the code, however it did not work, this was likely due to the two networks being on different subnets, however I had no way of testing as, at that point, the VM stopped networking properly and I could not longer access or configure settings on the VM.

This VM was strange for me as it was the first machine I have looked at where the clues are hidden in strange places like within images or the directories following some pattern to find other pages and while these were strange at the time, it isn’t entirely outside of the realm of possibility that real sites have similar cases.

Reflection

There were two free-for-alls during the week, one on Monday and the second on Friday. On Monday I spoke with Mitchell who echoed a lot of my experiences with SQL injection and writing XSS exploits, though in retrospect things like this could easily be searched for online if I kept better track of resources. There is no telling whether I offered him any interesting information, but I probably babbled on for a little too long.

In Friday’s free-for-all I was speaking with Chris, David, and Max, or more listening than anything. They were talking about the boxes they had managed to get root access on while I listened along and took notes on their process, for the most part, they gave me help with the machines I was trying to break into, showing me the sudo -l command for viewing a user’s sudo commands as well as how to output to a dump file in Metasploit.

I really feel I should have done more vulnerable machines, or at least tried something more difficult, I don’t expect that I would be able to anything more difficult on account of not being able to do PwnLab, but the experience and frustration might be worth at least a little bit.

The work I produced this week feels less than that of the previous week, but the quality to how I presented it is definitely higher, with regards to my presentation this is probably not a result of improvement, but just a change in approach and attitude, being more prepared and having resources on hand to best present my content.

The most important thing I took out of this week is the breadth of new tools I found to help with investigating and penetrating a system, meaning my attempts to infiltrate a system have become less simple and a bit better thought out. By no means do I think my process is any faster, but without a doubt, I have spent less time hitting my head against individual aspects and focused more on finding as much as possible.

Bug Bounties and Responsible Disclosure

The first task we were designated to complete by 13/2/19 was the research on the benefits of ethical hacking and bug bounties and how we can use the skills learned in the week to respond to responsible disclosure plans. The word limit was between 300 and 500 words with my write-up below.

According to CVEDetails (2019), there were a total of 16,555 publicly recorded vulnerabilities found in 2018, having increased exponentially from previous years.


The vast majority of these vulnerabilities focus around interfering with or gaining control of services on a system. Bug bounties are a form of crowd-sourced security testing offered by many large organisations and communities within the cybersecurity industry with the express purpose of encouraging the responsible disclosure of dangerous flaws before their impacts can be seen.


Responsible hacking through bug bounties is an effective way to minimise the costs of penetration testing and hiring of specialised staff by essentially contracting freelancers on a per-bug basis with payments as high as $250,000 for vulnerabilities in programs such as Microsoft Hyper-V (CRN 2018), where any slight bug could cost the company significantly.

In an industry where many experts are employed in contracted groups and less often to individual development teams (except in large organisations) bug bounties are a highly cost-efficient way of identifying possible entry points in both large and small scale products.

Responsible disclosure is a way to protect the reporter and developer when a bug or vulnerability is found. In the general sense, responsible disclosure policies permit the public to probe and test the security of programs and allow developers to respond to instances of reported bugs preemptively before issuing a notification to the general public.

A proper disclosure policy is important to encourage proactive testing of security formats on a system, companies such as Dropbox (Hackerone 2018) encourage such practices with a reasonably detailed disclosure policy and bounty program with well-defined bounds and scope to focus in on areas of concern for the company without disregarding the sensitive areas for users such as user details.


Understanding a particular organisation’s responsible disclosure agreement anyone could test their in-scope items for any vulnerability given it does not break the agreement. However, this does not mean any site or organisation can be investigated for vulnerabilities while staying within legal grounds, proper research into the available domains and whether an organisation has an agreement is paramount to legally testing systems as an outsider.

Web Vulnerability Research

The task of researching and testing a web application was due for completion on Friday 15/2/19, my chosen web-app was “Web for Pentester” (PentesterLab 2019) by PentesterLab, as per approval by the supervisors. Going in I knew this would be an easy application to research the vulnerability of and I chose in on account of this ease, knowing it would be within my current skill. I set myself the goal of seeing exactly how deep into the connected systems I could get using publicly available methods and vulnerabilities. I had two VMs for this situation, one running the Web for Pentester web application locally and another with a provided VM of Kali Linux for all my penetration testing purposes, my limitations to this were to not interfere with the contents of the web-app VM except for verification of attacks.

The VM itself was designed to be very vulnerable from a web-app perspective, so I used one entry point as a method to infiltrate the rest of the system as far as I could, the particular issue I focused on was that of the unrestrained file upload, which is a serious issue for a server as anything can be uploaded and potentially executed.

My first step in finding vulnerabilities in the system was to understand the layout of the configuration, I did this by first mapping the publicly facing ports using nmap. Doing this showed me the versions of configured software, as it was not hidden. I referenced these software versions on CVEDetails (CVEDetails 2019) to ascertain what potential vulnerabilities I could attack on the system.


The most vulnerable element of the provided was Apache 2.2.16, which had 27 vulnerabilities at the time with multiple different impacts, as my skill was low I looked for vulnerabilities with high impact and low complexity. The first I tried was a simple DOS using a vulnerability in how Apache handled the Range header with regards to overlapping byte values, as described in an article by SpiderLabs (SpiderLabs 2011), using a provided script I was able to replicate the attack somewhat.

On the Web for Pentester VM I looked at the network traffic using netstat before and during the attack, during the attack about 20 packets were coming in per second from my client. This didn’t seem very effective and I wasn’t too sure as to whether it was actually working, so I quickly gave up as I was seeing no results or change to CPU usage by the server.

My next avenue was to shift my attention towards attempting to gain root access on the machine, I did not have any method of achieving this so I mainly spent a lot of time researching. Using the file upload feature I uploaded a PHP script that would implement a rudimentary web shell (artyuum 2018) for use on the web-app. Using this shell I could input simple one-line commands to investigate the system and check if my changes were properly implemented in the future.

I first attempted to usermod the web user to have root privilege, this was of course not allowed, so I then tried to brute force the password for the root user’s ssh configuration using Hydra and the “rockyou” wordlist. After 2 hours of waiting I figured it would take too long and gave up, it was later pointed out by David that the estimated time of 2220 hours was likely due to the wordlist I had used, though it might not have made much of a difference.

In testing I found I could, for whatever reason, use chmod to change the permissions of files owned by the web user and execute them, this was important as it would allow me to execute scripts for further attacks on the application. Using uname -r I was able to obtain the application’s version of Linux (2.6.32), which I used as a reference to find vulnerabilities on Google. There were two that stuck out to me, a vulnerability in the pipe.c function and an exploit called “Dirty Cow”. As shells already existed online I simply downloaded and tested the scripts on the application, at first I tried the pipe.c exploit, but I could neither understand it or execute it properly, as such I shifted my attention to the latter exploit.

Dirty Cow (CVE 2016-5195) is an exploit in Linux based systems between version 2.x and 4.83 abusing how mm/gup.c handled copy-on-write to write to read-only memory, allowing a user to elevate their privilege. I used a script authored by Firefart on exploit-db (Firefart 2016) to do most of this for me, with the overall purpose being to write a new user to the /etc/passwd file that was read-only for the web user.

As the script was a .c file I had to compile it as a binary for execution, this had to specifically be in 32-bit as the target Linux system was a 32-bit system and did not support 64-bit binaries. Once that had been done I uploaded the script using the file upload on the web-app and executed the script using ./dirty password, which would replace the root user with one of a pre-configured username (which I did not change) and my desired password (password)

I could confirm the success of the script by viewing the /etc/passwd file, which showed my new user (firefart:password), I made sure the user had elevated privilege by first attempting to SSH into the user and then attempting to edit the /etc/passwd file, which worked.
Please ignore the username, I didn’t think to change it.



This was actually a lot of fun to do and took about 14 hours of work to research and execute, I tried a whole bunch of new things, most didn’t work, but was extremely excited when I finally had something to show for it. Until about the last hour I did not actually think anything would work and that the system was properly protected, but after a couple of tries I started to progress past the various hurdles in the way. All in all this was educational, exhausting and really nice to get done.
